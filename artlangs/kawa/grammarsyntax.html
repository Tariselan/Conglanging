Grammar and Syntax

Syntax

Kawa follows an SVO word order. The verbal in Kawa are rather isolating in nature wheras the noun phrases are much more agglutanative.



Nouns

Nouns decline for 3 cases, the cases are as follows:

  1. Nominative - used when the noun is the subject or the predicate nominative. The thing or person doing the acion: People love me: pesomo mwa imo.
  2. Accusative - used when the noun is the direct object. The thing being acted upon: I love people: imo mwa pesomo.
  3. Genitive - used when the noun is the possessor of or connected with an object: The cat's hair: hawamo gati; the agent of a passive verb: The person was injured by me: o goŋ knot pesomo iŋi; and the partitive:.
  4. Dative - used when the noun is the indirect object or being used in a comparison, certain verbs will also change meaning if the object is dative (tambi to thank, to give thanks to + DAT): I gave them a cat: imo goŋ dewo gato sovia; I am better than you: imo gaŋqe asimus ta.
Note that the nominative and accusative case are identical for all nouns and the distinction is only made in the derogatory pronouns.

Nouns indicate noun case by changing their ending. The ending of the noun depends on whether or not the base-form of the noun ends in a consonant (C) or a vowel (V). They decline as follows:

Nominative / Accusative Genitive Dative
-C -o -i -a
-V -mo -mi -ma

Articles

Kawa does have an indefinite and definite article, however, these are not common in day to day speech and they are only found in formal writing.

Definite Indefinite
te am


Pronouns

Pronouns declin for case, gender, and derogatress; derogatress is distinction between derogatory pronouns.
Pronouns decline as follows:

Nominative Accusative Dative Genitive
1st Person Plain imo imwa iŋi
Derogatory imwo imƿo ima iŋwi
2nd Person Plain teo ta da
Derogatory dƿeo gweo dam diwi
3rd Person Plain sov sovia sovio
Derogatory zof zov zia zio

Derogatory Pronouns and Derogatress

Take the sentence, "You are a nerd." The senteice isn't itself inherently considered an insult (or with having a derogatory sense), but in many dialects of Modern English, because of history and culture, it tends to be an insult. However, it needs not to be, and often its intent/derogatress is often shown/inferred using things such as context, tone, non-verbal language, etc etc. 

   You are a nerd. -> teo gaŋqe qiqamo
This sentence is merely a statement of fact without any intent of being an insult, moreso to describe. (LIT: you have the quality of a nerd)
Compare this with the sentence: 
   You are a nerd, -> dƿeo gaŋqe qiqamo
This sentence implies moreso an insulting tone, with the intent to insult the listener, whereas teo just implies a general statement, dƿeo implies a more insulting manner of delivery.

The derogatory and non-derogatory distinction is very similar to that of the T-V distinction found in a lot of romantic languages. Except here it is applier in a more general context in a slightly different manner.
NOTE: Although derogatory can be analysed as informal, the derogatory is often used in insulting, joking, et al manners which tend to happen in informal contexts. teo is still used in preference by Kawa speakers in informal contexts.



Verbs

Verbs do not inflect for tense, aspect, or mood, instead tense is indicated with the following particles:

Indicative Imperative Conditional
Present gaŋ qaŋ gagaŋ
Past goŋ - gogoŋ
Future kam kakam

Take the verb sda, meaning to eat, conjugated as follows:

  • imo sda - I eat
  • imo gaŋ sda - I am eating
  • imo gon sda - I ate / I was eating
  • imo kam sda - I will eat / I will be eating
  • imo gagaŋ sda - I would eat / I would be eating
  • imo gogoŋ sda - I would have eaten / I would have been eating
  • imo kakam sda - I would eat [in the future] / I would be eating [in the future]
  • (teo) qaŋ sda! - Eat!

The Copula

In Kawa, there is one main copula 'qe' which functions both as the verb 'to be' and the verb 'to have'. 'qe' is the only verb that inflects; it inflects such as the following table:

Indicative Imperative Conditional
Present gaŋqe qeqe! gaqegaŋ
Past goŋqe - goqegoŋ
Future kamqe kaqekam

Auxiliary verbs

Kawa uses auxiliary verbs to express different moods and aspects. Auxiliaries have two forms, one before verbs beginning with a consonant, and one that elides before a verb beginning with a vowel.
Auxiliary verbs expressing a Deontic Mood
Auxiliary verb Meaning English translation
sd(e) "should"

Auxiliary verbs expressing an Epistemic Mood
Auxiliary verb Meaning English translation
zƿ(e) "must"

Auxiliary verbs expressing a Dynamic Mood
Auxiliary verb Meaning English translation

Order of particles before a verb

The order of particles that can go before a verb go as the following: 

1. tense marker 2. negation marker 3. mood marker
For example:
imo kamqe no sde knot vo sov.
1P ind-Fu neg should fighht for 3P
I should not go fight for them.



Adjectives and Adverbs

In Kawa, there is no distinction between adjectives and adverbs. However, whenever a word is being used as an adjective, it goes before the noun. Whereas when it is being used as an adverb, it is used at the end of a sentence or after the verb.
